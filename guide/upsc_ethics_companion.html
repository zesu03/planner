What this paper is really for

It tests judgement, not recall

Other papers ask what you know. Ethics asks who you would be as an officer. The highest scorers don't have the rarest theory — they have a clear value-position, the discipline to structure it, and the maturity to weigh competing goods. Treat it as the most scoring paper, because it is the most teachable.

The two halves

Strategy
Section A is theory — define crisply, illustrate with an example, take a balanced view. Section B is ~6 case studies — act as the decision-maker in the scenario. Section B often decides your score, so practise cases hardest.

The golden rule

Show, structure, balance
Every answer: a crisp concept, a concrete example (a leader, scheme, or lived situation), and a balanced judgement. Keywords, diagrams and a clear stance beat long moralising paragraphs.

Be practical, not preachy

Tone
Examiners reward feasible, humane, lawful decisions over idealistic sermons. Acknowledge constraints, choose a course, and own the trade-offs. Sounding noble while ducking the decision loses marks.

Your example bank

Stock it
Keep ready: thinkers and their core idea, civil-service values with real instances, governance tools, and 2–3 personal-life examples. Specific, verified material beats generic virtue-words every time.

Section B — where the paper is won

A method for case studies

Don't write a story; run a process. The same six steps work for almost any case, from a bribe offer to a relief-allocation dilemma. Write the steps as labelled mini-paragraphs so the examiner sees the structure at a glance.

1

Name the core dilemma

State the central ethical conflict in a line — usually a clash of two legitimate values (a "right vs right"), not simply right vs wrong.

2

Map the stakeholders

List everyone affected and their legitimate interests — the citizen, the institution, your subordinates, your family, society at large, yourself.

3

Surface the values in conflict

Identify the principles pulling against each other — e.g. compassion vs rule of law, loyalty vs integrity, efficiency vs equity.

4

Lay out the options

Give 2–4 realistic courses of action, including the lazy and the heroic, so your final choice is visibly reasoned, not assumed.

5

Evaluate each option

Weigh each on consequences, legality, feasibility and an ethical lens (duty / outcome / virtue). Show the trade-offs honestly.

6

Decide, justify, and look forward

Commit to a course as the officer would, justify it with a value and a thinker if apt, and add a systemic fix so it doesn't recur.

Worked example — open each step

The factory, the jobs, and the river

The scenarioYou are the District Collector. A politically powerful industrialist seeks fast-track clearance for a factory that would create 2,000 local jobs. But it violates environmental norms and would discharge effluent into the only water source of a downstream tribal hamlet. The local MLA hints that your transfer depends on your "cooperation."
1 The core dilemma +
Economic development and employment (a real public good) versus environmental protection and the right to life and water of a vulnerable, voiceless community — sharpened by political pressure that pits your career against your conscience. This is a right-vs-right clash, layered over a right-vs-wrong temptation.
2 Stakeholders +
  • Tribal hamlet: health, livelihood, right to clean water — least powerful, most affected.
  • Job-seekers & local economy: legitimate interest in employment.
  • Industrialist & MLA: profit and political interest.
  • You / the administration: rule of law, public trust, personal integrity and career.
  • Future generations & the ecosystem: the silent stakeholders.
3 Values in conflict +
Development & utility vs environmental sustainability and intergenerational justice; compassion for the poor vs political loyalty; personal career security vs integrity, objectivity and the rule of law.
4 Options +
  • (a) Clear the factory as pressured — gains jobs and political favour, but breaks the law and harms the hamlet.
  • (b) Reject outright — protects the hamlet but forfeits jobs and invites retaliation.
  • (c) Conditional path — require effluent treatment, relocation of the discharge, and proper clearances before approval, so jobs and environment are both served.
5 Evaluation +
Option (a) fails on legality and on the duty to the weakest (it treats the hamlet as a means — a Kantian red line). Option (b) is lawful but a false binary that needlessly sacrifices a genuine good. Option (c) is lawful, feasible and reconciles utility with sustainability — the Gandhian test of recalling the poorest face is met, and the rule of law holds.
6 Decision & way forward +
Choose (c): grant clearance only on strict, enforceable environmental safeguards and statutory approvals, documented in writing. Resist the transfer threat through proper record-keeping and, if needed, escalation — integrity is not negotiable for career security. Systemically, push for transparent single-window clearance with mandatory environmental and social-impact audit, so the next officer never faces this pressure informally. The decision is humane, lawful, and durable.

The pattern to copy: reject false binaries, find the lawful third path that honours the weakest stakeholder, decide firmly, and fix the system. Resist threats without grandstanding.

How ethics answers bleed marks

The avoidable losses

Preaching instead of deciding. The commonest failure — noble paragraphs that never choose a course. Always commit to an action and own its trade-offs.
Idealism without feasibility. "Refuse the bribe and inspire everyone" ignores real constraints. Show how, lawfully and practically, you'd act.
Ignoring stakeholders. Skipping the voiceless party (the tribal hamlet, the subordinate, the future generation) drops the marks for completeness.
Generic virtue-words. "Honesty, integrity, transparency" repeated without a concept, example or thinker reads as padding. Be specific.
Not playing the role. In a case you are the officer named, with that officer's powers — not an outside commentator. Decide from inside the chair.
No systemic fix. Resolving only the immediate problem misses easy marks; add the reform that stops it recurring.
